The Space Debris Crisis
Understanding the problem from absolute zero — no prior knowledge needed
1.1 What is a Satellite?
A satellite is any object that orbits (travels around) the Earth. There are two kinds: natural satellites (like the
Moon) and artificial satellites — machines we launch into space. When people say 'satellite' in the context of
this project, they mean artificial satellites.
Satellites are launched into orbit by rockets. Once in orbit, they keep moving in a curved path around Earth
because of a balance between their speed (which tries to throw them away from Earth) and gravity (which
tries to pull them back down). This balance lets them orbit indefinitely without any fuel as long as they stay
above the atmosphere.
What do satellites do?
Use Case
How it helps people
GPS Navigation
Your phone knows where you are because GPS satellites beam their location constantly.
Weather Forecasting
Weather satellites take photos of clouds and storms from above.
Internet (Starlink)
SpaceX's Starlink constellation gives internet access to rural areas worldwide.
Military Surveillance
Governments use satellites to monitor borders and troop movements.
Scientific Research
NASA's Hubble telescope is a satellite that photographs distant galaxies.
TV Broadcasts
Dish TV works because satellites relay TV signals from studios to homes.
How many satellites are there?
As of 2024, there are roughly 9,000 active satellites in orbit. But the number is growing explosively —
SpaceX alone plans to have 42,000 Starlink satellites in orbit. Amazon, OneWeb, and dozens of other
companies are also launching thousands more. By 2030, experts expect over 100,000 satellites to be
operational simultaneously.
Key Fact
SpaceX launches up to 60 Starlink satellites in a single rocket. As of 2024, there are already over 6,000 Starlink
satellites in orbit — more than all other satellites combined.
1.2 What is Space Debris?
Space debris (also called space junk) is any man-made object in orbit that is no longer functional. Think of it
like litter — but orbiting Earth at 28,000 km/h.

How fast is space debris moving?
Objects in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) travel at approximately 7.8 kilometres per second — that is 28,000 km/h. A
1 cm piece of debris at this speed hits with the same energy as a bowling ball thrown at 90 km/h. A 10 cm
piece can completely destroy a satellite. There is no way to dodge an untracked piece of debris.
Why size doesn't matter much in space
A 1 cm aluminium ball at orbital velocity carries enough kinetic energy to penetrate the walls of the International
Space Station. At 28,000 km/h, a marble becomes a cannonball. This is why even small untracked objects are an
existential threat to satellites.

1.4 Why This Matters Right Now
The Kessler Syndrome, proposed by NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, describes a catastrophic chain
reaction: one collision creates debris, that debris causes more collisions, which create more debris, until
certain orbital shells become completely unusable — forever. No more GPS. No more weather satellites. No
more internet from space.
In 2009, the first accidental satellite collision in history happened: Iridium-33 (an active US communications
satellite) and Kosmos-2251 (a dead Russian military satellite) collided at 11.7 km/s, creating over 2,300
tracked fragments — which are still in orbit today. In May 2024, a severe G5 geomagnetic storm disrupted
GPS globally — a preview of what infrastructure loss looks like. The difference with a debris cascade is it
would be permanent.

2.1 Orbits Explained Simply
Imagine throwing a ball horizontally. It curves downward due to gravity and hits the ground. Now throw it
faster. It travels further before hitting the ground. Throw it fast enough (7.9 km/s), and the curve of its fall
exactly matches the curve of the Earth — it never hits the ground. It is now in orbit.
Orbits are described by their altitude (height above Earth) and shape. The closer to Earth, the faster you
must travel to stay in orbit, and the shorter your orbital period (time for one full revolution).

Why DebrisMind focuses on LEO
LEO is the most crowded orbital shell — this is where Starlink, OneWeb, the ISS, and most debris live. The
combination of high density and high relative speeds (up to 15 km/s during head-on approaches) makes LEO the
most dangerous environment for collision events.
2.2 TLE — Two-Line Element Sets
A TLE (Two-Line Element set) is the standard format used worldwide to describe where a satellite is in its
orbit and how it is moving. It is called 'two-line' because the data is packed into exactly two lines of text, 69
characters each. This format was invented by NORAD in the 1960s and is still used universally today.

2.3 SGP4 — The Physics Engine
SGP4 (Simplified General Perturbations 4) is a mathematical model — a set of equations — that takes a TLE
and calculates where a satellite will be at any future time. It accounts for the fact that orbits are not perfect:
the Earth is not a perfect sphere (it bulges at the equator), the atmosphere has trace amounts of drag even at
400 km altitude, and the Sun and Moon have tiny gravitational effects.
Think of SGP4 as the 'simulator' that runs time forward. You give it a TLE and say 'where will this satellite be
in 6 hours?' and it gives you an X, Y, Z position in 3D space and velocity (how fast it is moving in each
direction). This is not an approximation — it is the same model used by NORAD, NASA, and every space
agency on Earth.
SGP4 in practice
The Python library 'sgp4' (pip install sgp4) implements the full SGP4 model. It can compute a satellite's position and
velocity for any moment in time in under 1 millisecond on a standard laptop. For 200 satellites over a 7-day window
(sampled every 60 seconds), the full computation takes under 5 seconds.
2.4 Conjunction & TCA
A conjunction is when two objects in orbit come dangerously close to each other. 'Dangerously close' is
defined as coming within a threshold distance — typically 5 km for large satellites and 1 km for critical assets
like the ISS.
TCA stands for Time of Closest Approach — the exact moment when the two objects are at their minimum
distance from each other during a conjunction. This is the critical moment: if a maneuver is needed, it must
be completed before TCA.

2.5 Delta-V — The Currency of Space
Delta-V (written as delta-v, or dv) literally means 'change in velocity.' In space, every maneuver — speeding
up, slowing down, changing orbit — costs delta-V. Satellites carry a finite amount of propellant, and when it
runs out, the satellite is dead. Delta-V is measured in meters per second (m/s).
A typical collision avoidance maneuver costs 0.1 to 1.0 m/s of delta-V. A Starlink satellite carries enough fuel
for roughly 100–200 m/s of total delta-V over its lifetime. Each avoidance maneuver therefore consumes a
meaningful fraction of the satellite's fuel budget. This is why DebrisMind's multi-agent negotiation is valuable
— by choosing which satellite maneuvers, the system minimises total fuel expenditure across the
constellation.
2.6 Multi-Agent Systems
A multi-agent system (MAS) is a network of independent software programs (agents) that each perceive their
environment, make decisions, and take actions — and can communicate with each other. Think of it like a
group of robots that each have their own brain but can talk to each other and coordinate their actions.
In DebrisMind, each satellite is represented by a software agent. The agent knows its satellite's orbital state
(position, velocity, fuel level, mission priority) and can propose maneuver options. When two agents detect
that their satellites are on a collision course, they negotiate: who moves, when, how much, and in which
direction — minimising total fuel cost while guaranteeing safety.

3.1 Current State of Collision Avoidance
Today, collision avoidance works like this: the US Space Force's 18th Space Control Squadron continuously
tracks all objects larger than 10 cm. When their algorithms detect a conjunction, they send a Conjunction
Data Message (CDM) to the satellite operator — usually 2 to 7 days before the TCA. The operator's team
then manually analyses the CDM, decides whether to maneuver, calculates a safe burn, commands the
satellite to execute the burn, and waits for confirmation.
This process has three fatal flaws that will cause it to break down completely as constellation sizes grow from
hundreds to tens of thousands of satellites:
Problem

Impact

Each satellite acts alone

Coordination gap at the international level

DebrisMind introduces a multi-agent coordination layer that sits between raw orbital data and the operator's
command console. Instead of each satellite reacting independently, DebrisMind's agents communicate,
negotiate, and produce a globally optimal maneuver plan — autonomously, in minutes rather than days.

4.2 The Novel Idea: Orbital Lane Negotiation
The key innovation is treating the avoidance problem as a negotiation between agents, not a solo calculation.
Here is how it works conceptually:
Step 1: Two satellites are detected to be on a collision course. Miss distance: 1.2 km. TCA: 31 hours from
 now.
Step 2: Agent A (representing Sat-A) calculates three maneuver options it could perform — each with a
 different fuel cost and resulting miss distance.
Step 3: Agent B (representing Sat-B) does the same — three options on its side.
 
Step 4: Both agents submit their proposals to a Mediator Agent. Each proposal includes: proposed delta-V,
 resulting miss distance, fuel cost, and a natural-language justification ('My satellite is on a critical imaging
pass, proposing minimum burn').
Step 5: The Mediator Agent evaluates all combinations — which pair of proposals produces safe miss
 distance at minimum total fuel cost — and selects the winner.
Step 6: The winning plan is formatted as an operator brief and displayed on the dashboard. Maneuver is
 shown in 3D on the globe.
4.3 What the System Does End-to-End
Input: A live TLE feed for 200+ Starlink satellites and known debris objects. Output: For every detected
conjunction — a complete, human-readable operator brief with the recommended maneuver, fuel cost, new
miss distance, and 3D visualisation on a globe.

5.3 Conjunction Detector
The conjunction detector is the most computationally intensive component. For N satellites, there are
N*(N-1)/2 pairs to check — for 200 satellites, that is 19,900 pairs. For each pair, we compute the minimum
distance over a 10,080-point timeline (7 days × 24 hrs × 60 min). Using numpy vectorisation, this runs in 3–8
seconds on a standard laptop.

The algorithm: propagate both satellites to the same set of timestamps using SGP4. Compute the Euclidean
distance between their ECI (Earth-Centered Inertial) positions at each timestamp. Find the minimum. If the
minimum is below the threshold, record the pair, the TCA timestamp, and the miss distance.
ECI coordinates explained
ECI (Earth-Centered Inertial) is a 3D coordinate system with the origin at Earth's center, where the X axis points
toward a fixed star, not rotating with Earth. It is the natural coordinate system for orbital mechanics because
satellites follow straight-line physics in this frame — no pseudo-forces from Earth's rotation.

How the LLM is used
Each agent is implemented as a Python function that calls an LLM API (Groq, Gemini, or OpenAI). The LLM
receives a structured prompt containing the satellite's state as JSON and is instructed to return a JSON array
of maneuver proposals. The LLM's role is reasoning: deciding which maneuver direction makes physical
sense, weighing mission priority, and writing the justification text. The actual orbital mechanics (computing
the new trajectory after each proposed burn) is done by the SGP4 library — not the LLM.

6.4 How to Fetch and Parse in Python
import requests
from sgp4.api import Satrec, jday
# Fetch all Starlink TLEs
url = 'https://celestrak.org/SOCRATES/query.php?GROUP=starlink&FORMAT=tle'
lines = requests.get(url).text.strip().split('\n')
# Parse into satellite objects (every 3 lines = 1 satellite)
satellites = []
for i in range(0, len(lines)-2, 3):
name = lines[i].strip()
sat = Satrec.twoline2rv(lines[i+1], lines[i+2])
satellites.append({'name': name, 'sat': sat})
# Propagate one satellite to a specific time
from datetime import datetime, timezone
dt = datetime(2026, 6, 9, 14, 0, 0, tzinfo=timezone.utc)
jd, fr = jday(dt.year, dt.month, dt.day, dt.hour, dt.minute, dt.second)
e, r, v = satellites[0]['sat'].sgp4(jd, fr)
# r = [x, y, z] position in km (ECI), v = velocity in km/s

8.2 Step 1 — Data Pipeline (tle_pipeline.py)
File: backend/tle_pipeline.py. This fetches TLEs and propagates orbits.
import requests, numpy as np
from sgp4.api import Satrec, jday
from datetime import datetime, timedelta, timezone
def fetch_tles(group='starlink'):
url = f'https://celestrak.org/SOCRATES/query.php?GROUP={group}&FORMAT=tle'
lines = requests.get(url).text.strip().split('\n')
sats = []
for i in range(0, len(lines)-2, 3):
name = lines[i].strip()
sat = Satrec.twoline2rv(lines[i+1].strip(), lines[i+2].strip())
sats.append({'name': name, 'sat': sat})
return sats
def propagate(sats, hours=24, step_min=5):
times = [datetime.now(timezone.utc) + timedelta(minutes=i)
for i in range(0, hours*60, step_min)]
results = {}
for s in sats:
positions = []
for t in times:
jd, fr = jday(t.year,t.month,t.day,t.hour,t.minute,t.second)
e, r, v = s['sat'].sgp4(jd, fr)
if e == 0: positions.append(r) # e=0 means success
results[s['name']] = np.array(positions)
return results, times

import numpy as np
from itertools import combinations
def find_conjunctions(propagated, times, threshold_km=5.0):
conjunctions = []
names = list(propagated.keys())
for nameA, nameB in combinations(names, 2):
posA = propagated[nameA]
posB = propagated[nameB]
if len(posA) != len(posB): continue
# Vectorised distance computation (numpy — very fast)
diffs = posA - posB
distances = np.linalg.norm(diffs, axis=1)
min_idx = np.argmin(distances)
min_dist = distances[min_idx]
if min_dist < threshold_km:
conjunctions.append({
'satA': nameA, 'satB': nameB,
'miss_km': round(float(min_dist), 3),
'tca': times[min_idx].isoformat()
})
return sorted(conjunctions, key=lambda x: x['miss_km'])

import json
from groq import Groq # pip install groq
client = Groq(api_key='YOUR_FREE_GROQ_API_KEY')
def run_satellite_agent(sat_name, orbital_state, fuel_pct, priority, conjunction):
prompt = f'''You are the autonomous agent for satellite {sat_name}.
Orbital state: {json.dumps(orbital_state)}
Fuel remaining: {fuel_pct}% | Mission priority: {priority}
Conjunction: miss distance {conjunction['miss_km']} km at TCA {conjunction['tca']}
Propose exactly 3 maneuver options. Return ONLY valid JSON array:
[{{'delta_v_ms': float, 'direction': str, 'new_miss_km': float,
'fuel_cost_pct': float, 'justification': str}}, ...]'''
resp = client.chat.completions.create(
model='llama-3.1-70b-versatile',
messages=[{'role':'user','content':prompt}],
temperature=0.3
)
return json.loads(resp.choices[0].message.content)
def mediate(proposals_a, proposals_b, threshold=10.0):
best, best_cost = None, float('inf')
for pa in proposals_a:
for pb in proposals_b:
combined_miss = pa['new_miss_km'] + pb['new_miss_km']
if combined_miss >= threshold:
total_fuel = pa['fuel_cost_pct'] + pb['fuel_cost_pct']
if total_fuel < best_cost:
best_cost = total_fuel
best = (pa, pb)
return best

9.3 Anticipating Judge Questions
Judge asks
Your answer
'Is this real data?'
Yes — we fetch live TLEs from CelesTrak, the same source used by researchers worldwide. The positions are computed by SGP4, the same model used by NORAD.
'Is the collision real?'
The conjunction is real in that it is computed from real orbital data. We either find a real near-miss in the live data, or replay a known historical close approach.
'Why is the AI needed? Can't you just do the math?'
The math tells you THAT there is a risk. The AI agents decide WHO maneuvers, WHEN, and negotiates across multiple satellites to minimise total fleet fuel use — that's the novel part.
'Is this actually autonomous?'
Yes — from data fetch to conjunction detection to maneuver negotiation, no human is in the loop until the final Approve step. That is by design: operators stay in control of the final command.
'Could this scale to 42,000 Starlink satellites?'
The conjunction detector and agent architecture are both parallelisable. At full scale, this would run on cloud compute with parallel agent pools — the algorithms are the same.
